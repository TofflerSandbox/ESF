Key Principles

🔒 Shared ownership from day one. Every project needs a named co-sponsor before a dollar is obligated.

💡 Dual value paths. WCF fee-for-service (Path A) or cost avoidance in POM (Path B) — both are legitimate.

✂️ Project exit is success. A gate termination with documented rationale is the framework working as designed.

📅 PPBE-aware timing. Gate 3 must align to the POM calendar — not just technical readiness.

Purpose

Stage 0 is the intake filter. Before a single RDT&E dollar is obligated, three conditions must be met: the problem is real and DLA-mission-relevant; a functional directorate is co-owning from day one; and the team has declared which value path the solution will pursue — Path A (WCF fee-for-service revenue) or Path B (cost avoidance in POM baseline).

Gate 0 Exit Requirements
  • Functional co-sponsor named — J-code directorate, signed letter of intent, named POC
  • Problem statement scoped to a specific, measurable DLA operational pain point
  • Value path declared: Path A (WCF fee-for-service) or Path B (cost avoidance/POM line)
  • Rough order of magnitude for total lifecycle cost estimated
  • No disqualifying duplication with existing funded DLA programs confirmed
✅ Gate 0 — Go Decision

Project enters Phase I queue. J68 obligates up to $100k RDT&E. Co-sponsor commits to monthly engagement through Phase I.

◼ Project Exit Triggers

No functional co-sponsor willing to name a POC. Value path cannot be articulated. Problem is outside DLA mission scope. Duplicates a funded program.

📋 J68 PM — Your Action Items

Your job at Stage 0 is to sell the co-sponsor before you sell the idea. The technical concept matters less than whether you can get a J-code directorate to put their name on the problem. Without that, you have no gate passage. Identify your co-sponsor in week one, not week eight.

🤝 Co-Sponsor — What You're Agreeing To

Signing the Stage 0 letter of intent commits you to: monthly engagement through Phase I, attending Gate 1 review, and — critically — declaring whether you intend to eventually integrate this solution into your WCF Annual Operating Budget (Path A) or your POM submission (Path B). You are not yet committing funds. But you are committing intent.

🏛 Senior Leader — Policy Question at Stage 0

Who has authority to compel a directorate to co-sponsor a project they would prefer not to engage with? And what happens to high-priority projects that cannot find a co-sponsor? These questions must be resolved before the framework is chartered. See the Senior Leader Decisions tab.

🎯 Knowledge Check
A J68 PM presents a strong AI demand forecasting concept. JHU-APL has already signaled interest. J3 agrees it sounds valuable but declines to assign a named POC. What should happen?
✅ Correct. The co-sponsor requirement is a fundamental gate criterion — not a soft preference. Proceeding without one means J68 absorbs 100% of the risk and there is no commitment mechanism to prevent the pilot trap. A project without a co-sponsor is a project that will stall at Gate 3.
❌ Not quite. The co-sponsor is a pre-condition for any J68 funding — not something to be resolved during Phase I. Proceeding without one means J68 carries all the risk with no organizational commitment to eventual enterprise adoption. The WCF perverse incentive would be fully active from day one.

What the JHU-APL TRA Actually Tests

The TRA is not a rubber stamp. JHU-APL independently assesses whether the technology is mature enough to prototype, whether the proposed CONOPS is operationally realistic for DLA's environment, and whether identified capability gaps are resolvable within Phase II budget and timeline. A TRA "pass" requires all five components to be satisfactory.

✅ Gate 1 — Advance to Phase II

TRA confirms adequate readiness. Value hypothesis validated with real data. Co-sponsor reaffirms commitment. Cost model supports continued investment.

◼ Project Exit Triggers

TRA reveals fundamental technical barriers. CONOPS is operationally unrealistic for DLA. Hypothesis fails against operational data. Co-sponsor withdraws. Cost model breaks the business case.

📋 J68 PM — Managing the TRA

Schedule the JHU-APL engagement at project kickoff — not month 4. The TRA requires vendor documentation submissions, site visit coordination, and CONOPS development that take significant lead time. A Phase I that runs out of time before completing the TRA cannot achieve Gate 1 passage regardless of technical merit.

🤝 Co-Sponsor — Your Phase I Obligations

You are required to facilitate the vendor site visit to your DLA facility, participate in the CONOPS review, and attend the Gate 1 review. If the TRA reveals that the proposed solution doesn't actually fit your operational environment, it is far better to surface this at Gate 1 than at Gate 3 after $1M has been obligated.

🎯 Knowledge Check
JHU-APL completes the TRA and finds the technology is mature (TRL 6), but assesses the CONOPS as "operationally unrealistic" because it assumes a DLA staffing model that doesn't exist. The vendor wants to revise the CONOPS and resubmit. What should happen?
✅ Correct. An unrealistic CONOPS is a significant finding but not automatically terminal — it may reflect a fixable assumption gap rather than a fundamental operational mismatch. The right answer is to hold, fix, and revalidate. Advancing with a known CONOPS failure front-loads risk into Phase II where the cost of discovery is 10× higher.
❌ Reconsidering. The TRA assesses all five components as a package. TRL maturity alone doesn't compensate for an operationally unrealistic deployment concept — that's exactly the kind of failure the TRA is designed to catch before $1M is obligated. But a CONOPS revision is a correctable problem; it doesn't necessarily require termination.

⚑ Financial Path Must Be Named Before Gate 2

Path A: The solution will become a WCF service. Rate-setting methodology drafted. Operational directorate has indicated intent to include in Annual Operating Budget.

Path B: The solution delivers quantified cost avoidance. A specific POM program line has been identified and a sponsoring directorate SES has indicated intent to submit. PPBE calendar alignment has been verified.

✅ Gate 2 — Advance to Phase III

PRR passed. ROI case documented and certified by J8 Finance. Financial path named (A or B). Contracting, Finance, RM, J6 engaged. Co-sponsor reaffirms commitment to Gate 3.

◼ Project Exit Triggers

PRR fails. ROI insufficient to justify scaling cost. No financial mechanism identified. DLA architecture integration proves infeasible. Co-sponsor withdraws.

🤝 Co-Sponsor — The Path Declaration

By Gate 2, you must be prepared to state which financial path applies to your directorate. Path A means your Annual Operating Budget will eventually include this service as a WCF line item — customers (including your own users) will pay stabilized rates. Path B means your directorate will carry a POM program line. Neither is free. Neither can be deferred to "after we see if it works." The purpose of naming the path at Gate 2 is to give you maximum lead time before Gate 3's commitment deadline.

🎯 Knowledge Check
A Phase II PRR is completed in September. The project team wants to pursue Path B (POM program line). The DLA POM submission window is July–August. What is the soonest Gate 3 can open for this project?
✅ Correct. This is one of the most consequential PPBE calendar facts in the framework. A September PRR misses the July–August POM window by two months. Gate 3 cannot open for Path B until the following year's submission is confirmed — an 18+ month delay that risks losing co-sponsor momentum. This timing constraint must be identified and planned at Gate 0.
❌ This is the critical PPBE trap. The POM submission window is July–August. A September PRR misses it entirely. Gate 3 cannot open for Path B projects until the following year's POM submission is confirmed — meaning this project effectively waits 18+ months. See the WCF & PPBE tab for the full calendar view.

Phase III is the most critical gate in the entire framework. This is where the word "coordination" is replaced by the word "commitment." J6 sandbox connectivity is established. Multi-site deployment begins with real operational users. But none of this begins until the downstream funding owner has signed — in writing, at SES level — their commitment to Path A or Path B.

🔒 What "Commitment" Means — Path A vs Path B

Path A (WCF): The operational directorate's J8 Finance rep has confirmed inclusion of the solution's stabilized rate in the next Annual Operating Budget. The rate-setting methodology is documented. Customers have been notified they will be charged.

Path B (POM): A POM submission for this program line has been formally made. The sponsoring directorate SES has signed the program line justification. The PPBE submission is documented and date-stamped. "We plan to submit" is not sufficient.

⚠ The Perverse Incentive — Why This Gate Exists

WCF directorates have a rational short-term incentive to enjoy J68-funded pilots for free and decline ownership when the bill arrives at Gate 3. This gate prevents that dynamic. A directorate that signs a Stage 0 LOI and then declines at Gate 3 without documented cause should face a defined consequence — this is a Senior Leader policy decision (see Decision #7).

Phase III Requirements
  • J6 connectivity to DLA Cloud Sandbox / test network mirroring production — established before multi-site begins
  • Multi-site limited deployment executed with real operational users across at least two DLA facilities
  • All Phase I requirements (1–5) continue; systems engineering documentation maintained
  • Contracting, Finance, Resource Management, J6, and Operations formally documented as engaged
  • PBR (Product Baseline Review) and formal Sustainment planning initiated
  • ATO (Authority to Operate) plan approved by J6 — if not obtained in Phase II, this is now critical path
✅ Gate 3 — Advance to Phase IV

Funding commitment locked. Multi-site validated. Operational owner signed. J68 begins handoff preparation. ATO plan in place.

◼ Project Exit Triggers

No funding owner commits by POM deadline. Multi-site reveals unresolvable integration failures. Directorate formally declines. J6/ATO approval denied.

🏛 Senior Leader — This Gate Requires Your Authority

Gate 3 requires SES-level signatures from both J68 and the operational directorate. No PM-level actor can make this commitment. If the co-sponsor directorate declines to commit at Gate 3, only the DLA Director's office has the authority to either compel commitment or formally close the project. This is not a bureaucratic detail — it is the institutional mechanism that prevents the pilot trap.

🎯 Knowledge Check
At a Gate 3 review, the J4 co-sponsor says: "We love the solution and we're fully committed — we just need to finalize the POM numbers before we can sign. Can we proceed to Phase III and sign the commitment document next quarter?" What is the correct response?
✅ Correct. "We'll sign next quarter" is exactly the language that has caused every pilot trap in DLA's history. The commitment document is not a formality — it is the mechanism. A 30-day extension is reasonable; proceeding without the signature is not. If J4 is truly committed, signing takes a day. If signing proves impossible, the verbal commitment was not real.
❌ The signed commitment is the mechanism, not a formality. Verbal commitments at Gate 3 are the pattern that has caused DLA pilot projects to stall for years. A co-sponsor who cannot sign today will find equally compelling reasons not to sign next quarter. Hold the gate.

Gate 4 — Closeout Criteria
  • Operational owner formally accepts all program documentation, technical baseline, and sustainment responsibility
  • WCF rate is active and customers are placing orders, OR POM-funded program line is in execution
  • J68 has zero remaining contractual or financial obligations to the solution

✅ Gate 4 — Enterprise at Scale

Solution is self-sustaining. J68 declares program success and closes the portfolio entry. Annual performance reviews against original Gate 0 value path declaration.

✕ Descope Option

If full enterprise deployment is infeasible, the solution may be descoped to a subset of DLA. Partial success is formally documented, credited, and feeds the lessons learned database.

🎯 Knowledge Check
At Gate 4, the operational owner says they want J68 to retain technical ownership for another year "just in case." J68 has no more RDT&E budget for this project. What should happen?
✅ Correct. The handoff package — technical baseline, design decisions, known risks, operational runbook — is specifically designed to transfer institutional knowledge without retaining J68 dependency. Keeping J68 engaged beyond Gate 4 without budget authority creates an unfunded obligation and delays the portfolio entry from being closed. Solve the owner's confidence problem with documentation, not with continued J68 ownership.
❌ The design intent of Gate 4 is a clean handoff. J68 retaining ownership without budget authority creates an unfunded obligation. And treating owner hesitation as a project exit trigger misses the point — this is a documentation and communication problem, not a fundamental failure of the solution.

Which path fits your project?
Answer three questions to determine whether your solution should pursue Path A (WCF service) or Path B (POM cost avoidance)
1. When the solution is at scale, will external customers (military departments, other agencies) pay per-use for something DLA delivers?
YesDLA will operate a service and charge customers a stabilized rate per transaction, item processed, or service rendered.
NoThe solution makes DLA's internal operations more efficient but doesn't create a new revenue-generating service.
2. Can the ongoing operating cost of the solution be recovered through those customer charges without General Fund appropriations?
YesCustomer volume and rates will cover direct costs, overhead, G&A, and capital recovery without needing a POM program line.
No / UncertainOperating the solution requires General Fund appropriations because the customer base or volume is insufficient to break even.
3. Is the efficiency gain or cost avoidance quantifiable in dollars and attributable to a specific DLA budget baseline?
YesWe can document the before/after cost difference, attribute it to a budget line, and sustain that saving in the POM baseline.
No / Qualitative onlyThe benefit is real but hard to quantify in dollar terms — faster response time, fewer errors, improved data quality.
→ Path A: WCF Fee-for-Service

Your solution creates a new DLA service offering. The operational directorate includes the stabilized rate in its Annual Operating Budget. Customers order the service; their payments replenish the WCF corpus. Rate-setting begins ~18 months before the first execution year. Key milestone: J8 Finance must approve the rate methodology before Gate 3.

How the rate is built:
Direct costs (labor, materials, contracts)Base
+ Indirect/overhead (allocated proportionally)+%
+ G&A (DLA-wide costs across all activity groups)+%
± Prior-year gain/loss recovery (10 USC 2208)±Adj
= Stabilized rate (charged to customers)Fixed rate
→ Path B: POM Cost Avoidance

Your solution reduces DLA's operating costs and those savings must be documented in the POM baseline — effectively reducing the funding request in future cycles. The co-sponsor directorate SES must carry a POM program line justification and submit it in the July–August window. Key constraint: the PRR must complete by May at the latest to make that cycle.

→ Consult J8 Finance — May require a hybrid approach

Your solution's benefits are real but the financial mechanism isn't clear-cut. This is a conversation for J8 Finance and your co-sponsor's resource management team. Some solutions have both a WCF service component (Path A) and a cost avoidance component (Path B) — for example, a disposition automation system that creates a new per-item processing service and reduces the labor cost in the baseline.

Real Example: RFID Asset Tracking — Buy 100 or 10,000?
How procurement volume decisions flow through Path A vs. Path B

DLA Distribution wants to pilot RFID-enabled asset tracking at a single depot. The vendor quotes $85/tag at 100 units and $12/tag at 10,000 units. The technology works in Phase II. Now what?

PATH A SCENARIO: WCF Service
Tags purchased (10,000 @ $12)$120,000
Reader infrastructure (4 depots)$80,000
Software license (annual)$45,000
Labor (2 FTEs to manage)$180,000
Overhead (35%)$148,750
G&A (12%)$69,450
Total annual cost~$643,200
÷ 50,000 assets tracked/year$12.86/asset
Path A outcome: $12.86 becomes the WCF stabilized rate. Military departments pay per asset tracked. Tags are DLA capital, replaced as they cycle out. J4 sets this rate in the Annual Operating Budget and charges customers via the standard WCF billing mechanism.
PATH B SCENARIO: POM Cost Avoidance
Current labor cost (manual inventory)$940,000/yr
Error/rework cost (2.3% error rate)$215,000/yr
Baseline cost (current state)$1,155,000
RFID system total annual cost$425,000
Buy 100 tags @ $85 (pilot only)+$8,500 one-time
Annual cost avoidance$730,000/yr
Path B outcome: $730,000 in documented annual savings enters the POM baseline — J4 requests $730K less in the next POM cycle. The 100-tag pilot proves the savings model; enterprise deployment buys 10,000 @ $12 under a procurement line in the POM.
The key procurement insight: For Path A, tag cost feeds into the rate — higher volume = lower rate, better for customer adoption. For Path B, the capital cost of procurement (buying 10,000 tags) must appear in the POM as a one-time procurement line, while the recurring savings must appear in the baseline reduction. Both require J8 Finance to validate the cost model before Gate 2 closes.

The Structural Mismatch — Why This Framework Had to Be Built

J68 funds pilots with color-restricted, use-or-lose RDT&E dollars. The operational directorates run on the WCF — a revolving model where spending is driven by what they order. There is no natural handoff mechanism between these two financial worlds. A technically successful pilot must be deliberately translated into either a WCF service or a General Fund program line. Without that translation, it remains in the J68 portfolio indefinitely.

The WCF Perverse Incentive — What Gate 3 Solves

WCF directorates have a rational short-term incentive to enthusiastically co-sponsor pilots during Phases I–II (when J68 pays all costs) and then decline ownership at Gate 3 (when they would have to pay stabilized rates or carry a POM line). Gate 3's signed commitment requirement — which must precede Phase III funding — is the specific mechanism designed to prevent this behavior.

⚠️ Scenario C: The Pilot Trap — How It Happens
A technically successful pilot that stalls for three years and never reaches enterprise scale. Every failure mode this framework is designed to prevent.
Predictive Maintenance AnalyticsNo Path DeclaredWeak Co-SponsorWCF Incentive Failure
Stage 0 · Month 1
Intake — J4 agrees to co-sponsor informally
J68 PM presents a compelling predictive maintenance analytics solution. J4 expresses enthusiasm but does not assign a named GS-15 POC — the SES "verbally supports the effort." Value path is noted as "TBD — pending Phase I results." Red flag: Under this framework, this project does not pass Gate 0. It proceeds anyway under the old process.
Problem · Month 6
TRA passes — but no one owns the CONOPS
JHU-APL completes the TRA favorably (TRL 6). But the CONOPS review reveals the document was written by the vendor without J4 operational input. It describes a staffing model that doesn't match DLA's maintenance workforce structure. JHU-APL recommends a revised CONOPS. J4's informal POC has rotated out. No one at J4 picks up the thread.
Phase II · Month 8
Advances to Phase II without a revised CONOPS — $850k obligated
Under program pressure, the project advances to Phase II. The CONOPS gap is documented as "to be resolved during prototype." PRR is completed in October (missing the POM window). Path B is identified but no SES has agreed to carry the POM line. The project team assumes J4 will "come around" once they see the prototype results.
Failure · Gate 3
J4 declines to commit — "budget environment is constrained"
At the Gate 3 review, J4 explains they cannot commit a POM line in the current budget environment. They suggest J68 "continue to support the solution for another year while we socialize it internally." J68 has no RDT&E budget remaining for this project. The project enters a state of de facto suspension. Neither J68 nor J4 formally terminates it. It sits in the portfolio for 18 months consuming PM oversight time with no forward progress.
Termination · Month 54
Project formally closed — $1.1M in RDT&E with no enterprise impact
After 54 months and $1.1M in RDT&E, the project is formally terminated with no enterprise deployment. The technology worked. The vendor's solution was sound. The failure was entirely organizational: no binding co-sponsor commitment, no declared value path, no fiscal law-compliant funding mechanism for scale, and a PPBE calendar misalignment that was never corrected.

Under this framework: This project would have been an exit decision at Gate 0 (no named POC, no value path) — saving $1.1M in RDT&E for projects with genuine co-sponsor commitment.

Senior Leader Decision Points

11 Decisions That Only Senior Leaders Can Make

These are not process design questions — they are organizational policy, authority, and resource questions that require SES or GOFO-level resolution before this framework can be formally implemented. Click any decision to expand the analysis and options.

⚖️
Governance & Authority
1
Who has gate review authority — and can they veto a peer directorate?
Governance · Authority · Binding decisions
Critical▼

The framework requires a gate review body with genuine authority to stop projects at each gate. Can the J68 Director unilaterally terminate a project that a peer directorate wants to continue? Can J68 compel a directorate to accept co-ownership? Without clear authority architecture, gate reviews become advisory briefings — the current state the framework is designed to replace.

Option AEstablish a formal J68 Portfolio Review Board chaired by the DLA Deputy Director, with SES representation from each functional directorate. Gate decisions are binding at the 2-star equivalent level.
Option BJ68 Director retains sole gate authority for Gates 0–2; a cross-directorate Senior Investment Review Board governs Gates 3–5 where funding commitments are required.
Option CGate reviews remain advisory; framework operates on consensus. Lowest friction but highest risk of pilot trap recurrence.
What's at stake: Without binding gate authority, the project exit mechanism is meaningless. Projects stall indefinitely rather than receive documented termination.
2
Does the DLA Director need to formally charter this framework?
Authority · Policy · Charter
Critical▼

The framework's most powerful mechanisms require other directorates to do things they are not currently required to do — co-sponsor LOIs, mandatory Gate 3 commitment documents, binding project exit decisions. J68 cannot mandate this behavior unilaterally. A Director-level charter or policy memo is likely required to give the framework teeth across the enterprise.

Option ADLA Director issues a policy directive establishing this as the governing process for all J68-funded R&D projects, requiring directorate participation at each gate.
Option BJ68 implements for new projects only using existing authorities. Participation is voluntary but incentivized through preferential access to J68 funding.
Option CPilot the framework with 3–5 selected projects and build the evidence base before seeking Director-level charter in Year 2.
What's at stake: The enforceability of every co-sponsor commitment mechanism in the framework. Option B and C are lower risk but may produce a two-tier portfolio.
3
What legal standing do co-sponsor commitment documents have?
Legal authority · Fiscal law · OGC review required
High▼

Gate 3 requires a signed funding commitment from the operational directorate. The legal question is whether J68 can condition continued R&D funding on this commitment, and whether the commitment instrument has any legal standing. DLA OGC should review whether the co-sponsor LOI and Gate 3 commitment documents constitute enforceable instruments under fiscal law.

Option ADLA OGC reviews and approves a standard commitment instrument that creates a documented management obligation — not a legal contract, but a formal record informing future budget decisions.
Option BCommitment letters are management documents only. Rely on leadership culture and portfolio transparency for accountability rather than legal instruments.
What's at stake: If a directorate can sign a Gate 3 commitment and walk away without consequence, the entire gate structure is undermined. This requires an OGC opinion before formal charter.
💰
Financial Policy & Resource Decisions
4
Who has authority to establish a new WCF stabilized rate for a scaled J68 solution?
WCF · Rate-setting · J8 Finance authority
Critical▼

Path A solutions require a new stabilized WCF rate. Rate-setting is a formal budget process involving OSD Comptroller, DLA J8, and the relevant activity group manager. J68 cannot set rates unilaterally. The question is who initiates the process, what timeline it requires (typically 18 months before execution year), and whether J68 has a role in the cost methodology.

Option AOperational directorate adopting the solution owns rate-setting. J68 provides Phase II cost documentation as an input but has no ongoing rate authority.
Option BJ68 and operational directorate jointly develop rate methodology in Phase III, with J8 Finance as approving authority. Rate finalized before Gate 4.
What's at stake: If rate-setting is left entirely to the adopting directorate post-Gate 4, the timeline may not reflect J68's cost documentation, creating a gap between projected and actual sustainability.
5
Can J68 legally use RDT&E bridge funding in Phase III — and for how long?
Color of money · Fiscal law · Antideficiency Act risk
Critical▼

Using RDT&E appropriations to fund what is increasingly an operational deployment in Phase III creates fiscal law risk. The question is what Phase III activities remain RDT&E-eligible (integration testing, validation) vs. those that require O&M or WCF funding. This is not a PM-level judgment — it requires an OGC and J8 Finance opinion.

Option ADLA OGC and J8 Finance define a clear list of Phase III activities that remain RDT&E-eligible. Bridge capped at 12 months. Operational activities must shift to co-funding immediately.
Option BJ68 bridge funding prohibited in Phase III. Operational directorate must begin funding before Phase III opens — creating a harder but legally cleaner transition.
What's at stake: Potential Antideficiency Act exposure. This requires a legal opinion before the framework is chartered. Non-negotiable.
6
How does J68 triage 200+ active projects under this framework?
Portfolio management · Prioritization · Resource allocation
High▼

Not all 200+ active projects can receive equal gate review attention or bridge funding eligibility. The framework requires a portfolio scoring and triage strategy — which projects get expedited gate reviews, which receive Phase III bridge funding, and which are administratively closed if they cannot find co-sponsors within a defined window.

Option AWeighted scoring rubric at Gate 0: strategic alignment to DLA Director priorities, co-sponsor probability, strength of value case, time-to-enterprise. Top quartile receives priority review and bridge funding eligibility.
Option BAll projects receive identical gate treatment. First-come, first-served within each gate queue. Simple, fair, but may not align scarce bridge funding to highest-value projects.
Option CApply the framework only to new projects. Existing 200+ are grandfathered and managed under current approaches, avoiding disruptive retroactive reclassification.
What's at stake: Whether the framework becomes an enterprise operating model or a boutique process for a few high-visibility projects. Enterprise scope requires significant governance overhead investment.
🏗
Organizational Design & Incentives
7
How does DLA prevent the WCF perverse incentive at Gate 3?
Incentives · WCF structure · Accountability mechanism
Critical▼

WCF directorates have a rational incentive to engage enthusiastically in Phases I–II (J68 pays) and decline ownership at Gate 3 (they would have to pay). Leadership culture alone is insufficient. The incentive structure must be redesigned. This is a senior leadership policy decision — no PM can solve this.

Option ARequire directorates to cost-share Phase II funding (even 10–20%) as a skin-in-the-game mechanism. A directorate that has already spent money has stronger incentive to see it scale.
Option BEstablish a "decline penalty": any directorate that formally declines ownership at Gate 3 for a project they co-sponsored at Gate 0 is disqualified from co-sponsoring new J68 projects for one cycle.
Option CDLA Director publicly reviews Gate 3 commitment decisions annually. Transparency to senior leadership creates social accountability without formal penalties.
What's at stake: The entire Phase III mechanism. Routinely co-sponsoring at Gate 0 and walking away at Gate 3 turns J68 into an expensive feasibility study factory with no enterprise impact.
8
Should J68 retain any post-Gate 4 role — and how is it funded?
Post-closeout · Knowledge management · Technical advisor
Medium▼

J68 holds institutional knowledge of the solution's development history, technical risks, and design decisions. If the operational owner encounters problems post-scale, there is no defined mechanism to re-engage J68 expertise. Additionally, J68's Stage 5 annual portfolio reporting requires ongoing data from operational owners — which needs a formal information-sharing agreement.

Option AJ68 retains a "technical advisor" role for 24 months post-Gate 4, funded by the operational owner at a negotiated rate.
Option BClean break at Gate 4. Comprehensive handoff package. Post-closeout questions handled through normal DLA support channels.
What's at stake: Post-scale solution quality and the risk that operational owners, lacking J68's technical context, make changes that undermine solution effectiveness.
9
How does this framework interact with J6 ATO and DITAP governance?
IT governance · ATO · DITAP · Cybersecurity
High▼

DLA's IT governance — J6 ATO process, DITAP, cybersecurity requirements — must be formally integrated. ATO timelines (12–18 months) mean J6 must be engaged in Phase I or early Phase II for any technology-dependent solution. The framework currently treats J6 sandbox as a Phase III requirement — a potential mismatch.

Option AJ6 is added as a required gate participant beginning at Gate 1. ATO timeline is incorporated into the project schedule. No project advances to Phase III without an ATO plan approved by J6.
Option BJ6 engagement remains a Phase III entry requirement. Projects that cannot obtain ATO are project exit at Gate 3. Keeps early gates lean but accepts late-stage failure risk.
What's at stake: The most common late-stage failure mode in DLA technology projects is J6 or cybersecurity blockers that weren't anticipated early enough. Option A adds process burden but dramatically reduces Gate 3 failures.
🚀
Implementation & Change Management
10
Does this framework apply retroactively to the existing 200+ project portfolio?
Implementation · Transition · Existing portfolio
High▼

Applying retroactively requires gate-mapping every project, identifying co-sponsors for projects that never had them, and potentially cutting bait on a large cohort simultaneously — creating organizational turbulence. New intake only delays enterprise-wide impact by years.

Option ANew intake only. Framework governs all projects initiated after a defined go-live date.
Option BRetroactive with 6-month grace period. All existing projects must identify co-sponsor and declare value path or be administratively closed. High disruption, high impact.
Option CTiered: apply to existing projects approaching their next natural gate (next 12 months) and all new intake. Balances continuity with enterprise adoption.
What's at stake: The pace of portfolio rationalization and the political capital required for implementation.
11
What metrics prove this framework is working — and who reports to whom?
Metrics · Accountability · POM defense
Medium▼

Candidate metrics: % of projects reaching Gate 3 with locked funding commitment; average time Gate 0 to enterprise scale; dollar value of WCF revenue or cost avoidance per dollar of RDT&E invested; project exit rate by gate; and number of enterprises deployments achieved annually. Without agreed metrics, the framework cannot be defended in future POM cycles.

Option AJ68 publishes an annual Portfolio Performance Report to the DLA Director, tracking all metrics across the full portfolio. Reviewed in Director's annual performance cycle.
Option BMetrics tracked internally by J68 and reported quarterly to co-sponsors of active projects. Less visibility but lower overhead.
What's at stake: J68's ability to demonstrate return on the $250M portfolio in future POM cycles. Option A creates the strongest basis for budget defense.

Glossary

Key terms, acronyms, and concepts throughout the DLA J68 Enterprise Scaling Framework. Search to filter.

Working Capital Fund (WCF)
DLA's primary operating fund for its three activity groups: Supply Management, Energy, and Document Services. Operates as a revolving fund — customers order, DLA delivers and bills at stabilized rates, proceeds replenish the fund. Break-even goal under 10 USC 2208. No fiscal year limitation on the corpus.
Financial
PPBE / POM Cycle
Planning, Programming, Budgeting, and Execution. J68 submits its Program Objective Memorandum (POM) approximately every four years to compete for RDT&E appropriations. Path B projects must align Gate 3 timing to the POM submission window (typically July–August) or wait a full cycle — often 18+ months.
Financial
RDT&E Appropriation
Research, Development, Test & Evaluation. J68's primary funding color for Phases I–III. 2-year availability (use-or-lose). Cannot fund O&M, procurement, or sustainment activities. Color-of-money compliance is a legal requirement. Using RDT&E for operational activities creates Antideficiency Act exposure.
Financial
Stabilized Rate (WCF)
The fixed price DLA charges WCF customers, set during the annual budget process and held constant during the execution year. Must recover all direct, indirect, and G&A costs. Adjusts in future years to recover prior-year gains or losses. Mid-year changes require OSD Comptroller coordination and are rare. Rate-setting begins ~18 months before execution year.
Financial
TRA — Technology Readiness Assessment
Independent technical evaluation performed by JHU-APL as a required Phase I deliverable. Five components: CTE identification, CONOPS review, capability gap assessment, design document evaluation, requirements review. All five must be satisfactory for Gate 1 passage. Results are the primary Gate 1 evidence base.
Process
Path A vs Path B
The two legitimate financial scaling mechanisms. Path A: solution becomes a WCF fee-for-service offering, sustained by customer orders at stabilized rates. Path B: solution delivers quantified cost avoidance captured as savings in the POM baseline as a General Fund program line. Must be declared at Gate 0 and formally committed (signed) at Gate 3.
Financial
Project Exit Decision
Formal project termination at a gate, with documented rationale. Treated as the framework working correctly — not a project failure. Criteria are pre-defined at each gate. Frees J68 resources for higher-priority projects with genuine co-sponsor commitment. Project exit rationale is logged and reviewed for portfolio learning and informs future Gate 0 intake criteria.
Process
Functional Co-Sponsor
The named operational directorate (J3, J4, J6, etc.) that co-owns a project from Gate 0 onward. Must provide a signed LOI at intake — verbal support is insufficient. Responsible for committing WCF rate inclusion or POM program line at Gate 3. SES-level representative must attend Gate 3 review and sign the commitment document.
Governance
SE Milestone Reviews
Systems Engineering reviews required in Phase II: System Requirements Review (SRR), Preliminary Design Review (PDR), Critical Design Review (CDR), Test Readiness Review (TRR), and Product Readiness Review (PRR). The PRR is the terminal Phase II milestone, triggers the Gate 2 decision, and must include certification of the ROI model by J8 Finance.
Process
Gate 3 — Funding Commitment Gate
The framework's analog to JCIDS Milestone B. No project enters Phase III without a locked, signed funding commitment at SES level: WCF rate in AOB (Path A) or confirmed POM submission (Path B). "We plan to commit" is not sufficient. The signature is the mechanism — not a formality. The most important gate in the framework.
Governance
WCF Corpus
The initial working capital Congress provides to start a WCF activity. Operates like a business checking account — customer payments replenish it after the corpus fronts the cost of goods and services. Enables lead-time purchasing for customers before their orders arrive. Must maintain positive balance per OSD Comptroller guidelines.
Financial
ATO — Authority to Operate
J6's cybersecurity approval required for any IT system to connect to DLA production networks. Timeline typically 12–18 months. ATO planning should begin no later than Phase I (Gate 1) for any technology-dependent solution. Late J6 engagement is the most common cause of Gate 3 failures in DLA technology programs. J6 sandbox connectivity is required before Phase III opens.
Process
PBR — Product Baseline Review
Review conducted within Phase II or III to establish the product baseline and initiate formal sustainment planning. Triggers required coordination with Operations, Contracting, Finance, Resource Management, and J6. A PBR is a prerequisite for enterprise transition planning. Distinct from the PRR (which gates Phase II exit).
Process
CONOPS
Concept of Operations. Describes how a proposed solution will be used in DLA's operational context. Must reflect DLA's actual staffing model, workflow, and data environment — not the vendor's commercial use case. Reviewed by JHU-APL as a TRA component. An "operationally unrealistic" CONOPS finding at Gate 1 is a hold, not necessarily a project exit — it can be corrected before re-review.
